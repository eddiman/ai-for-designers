## Interview 1 — Maja (daily commuter, no car)

### Generic prompt
<!-- "Summarize this interview." -->

**Summary**

## 🧑‍💼 Participant Overview

* **Name:** Maja, 29
* **Occupation:** Marketing coordinator
* **Transport habits:** Daily bus user (commute + errands), no car
* **Context:** Relies entirely on public transport

***

## 🧭 Current Bus Experience

* Uses **fixed, repetitive routes** (home → centre → office; plus gym/social trips in evenings).
* Morning routine is **complex and mentally taxing**:
  * Checks multiple sources (app, live map, memory).
  * Does not fully trust real-time data.
  * Leaves earlier than necessary to avoid uncertainty.
* Evening travel is **less predictable**, requiring more flexible routing.

***

## ⚠️ Key Pain Points

### 1. Lack of Trust in Information

* Real-time data is unreliable (e.g. buses shown as arriving in 2 minutes that never come).
* Biggest frustration is **uncertainty**, not delays.
  * “Late” is manageable.
  * “Not knowing” creates stress and poor decisions.

### 2. Cognitive Overload

* Must manually reconstruct the same journeys every day.
* Relies on memorized patterns and workarounds (“the :08 bus is unreliable”).

### 3. Poor App Experience

* Apps feel:
  * **Generic**
  * Designed for **tourists, not habitual users**
* Constant need to:
  * Re-enter routes
  * Search manually
* No persistent memory or personalization (“assistant with amnesia”)

### 4. Reactive Instead of Proactive Tools

* Requires her to check information instead of being notified.
* No meaningful or actionable notifications.

***

## 💡 Desired Solution (Core Needs)

### 1. Personalization

* App should:
  * Learn frequent routes (home, work, gym, etc.)
  * Automatically recognize routines (e.g. weekday commute)
* Avoid repeated input: “never type a route again”

### 2. Proactive Guidance

* Smart notifications such as:
  * “Leave now”
  * “Your usual bus is delayed”
  * Suggested alternatives
* Context-aware (time, location, calendar)

### 3. Reliable, Actionable Information

* Clear updates on:
  * Delays
  * Cancellations
  * Crowding levels
* Provide **next best option immediately**

### 4. User Control

* Customizable notification rules:
  * Only weekdays
  * No unnecessary alerts

***

## 🧠 Underlying Insight

* Maja’s core problem is **mental load management**:
  * She is effectively “running a transport system” in her head.
* The opportunity is not infrastructure, but **information experience**.

***

## 🌟 Ideal Experience (Future Vision)

* App knows:
  * Day, routine, and destination
* Provides:
  * One-tap trip confirmation
  * Accurate timing and live adjustments
* Example:
  * “Leave in 4 minutes. Take 12 → 4. Arrive 08:52. Bus is busy.”
* Seamless, automatic, and trustworthy.

***

## 🎯 Key Takeaways

* **Trust > punctuality** — reliable info matters more than perfect timing
* **Personalization is critical** — daily riders need tailored tools
* **Proactive beats reactive** — users want guidance, not lookup
* **Consistency matters** — repetitive journeys should be frictionless

***

✅ **Bottom line:**  
Maja wants a **smart, personalized transit assistant** that removes uncertainty, reduces mental effort, and adapts to her routine—transforming commuting from stressful to effortless.

## Interview 2 — Geir (occasional rider, drives otherwise)

### Generic prompt
<!-- "Summarize this interview." -->

**Summary**


### Summary of Interview with Geir

**Bus usage & behavior**

* Geir uses the bus **about three times a week**, mainly to go into the city centre to avoid **expensive and difficult parking**.
* He follows a **simple, repetitive routine**: always the same route (Bus 9), same stop, similar times.
* He prefers **low-effort, habit-based travel**, not planning complex journeys.

**How he checks the bus**

* Relies on **physical information at stops** (paper timetable and electronic display).
* Has **little interest in mobile apps** due to perceived complexity (accounts, notifications, setup).
* His ideal interaction is minimal: **quickly see the next departure and move on**.

**Key frustration**

* The biggest issue is **unreliable real-time information**, not waiting itself.
* Electronic signs often show inaccurate countdowns or buses that never arrive.
* This creates a **loss of trust**, which he finds more frustrating than delays.

**Critical incident**

* On one occasion, a **diversion caused major delays**, but:
  * No clear information was provided at the stop
  * The display continued showing incorrect times
* He felt **“kept in the dark”**, and would have chosen another option (driving) if informed.

**What he wishes existed**

* Above all: **accurate, truthful, and transparent information**
  * Clear updates like: *delayed, cancelled, diverted*
* He does **not want extra features** (notifications, personalization, etc.)
* Prefers **simple, reliable systems over “clever” or feature-heavy solutions**

**Core insight**

* For Geir, the value of the bus system hinges on **trustworthy basic information**.
* If the system consistently told the truth, **most of his frustration would disappear**.

## Interview 3 — Sofie (student, mixed-mode traveller)

### Generic prompt
<!-- "Summarize this interview." -->

**Summary**

* **Usage pattern:** Sofie treats the bus as a **situational backup** rather than a primary mode—she uses it when it’s raining, late, or dark, but otherwise prefers walking or cycling.

* **Planning behavior:** She **plans last-minute using an app**, checking the next departure rather than preparing ahead.

* **Core frustration:** **Unreliable real-time information** is her biggest pain point. A past incident where a bus never arrived (“ghost bus”) caused lasting distrust. She now **overcompensates by catching earlier buses** and building in extra buffer time.

* **Emotional impact:** The uncertainty creates **stress and low-level anxiety**, especially because she can’t distinguish between a delay and a cancellation.

* **Primary need:** **Accurate, trustworthy timing information.** She says this alone would significantly improve her experience and reduce stress.

* **Desired feature:** A **simple, proactive cancellation alert** (e.g., push notification when her bus isn’t running). She values this because it prevents wasted waiting time.

* **What she doesn’t want:**
  * Complex personalization features (saved routes, profiles)
  * Frequent notifications
  * “Smart” or feature-heavy apps  
    She prefers **simple, reliable tools over sophisticated ones**.

* **Context-dependent needs:**
  * **Familiar routes:** Minimal guidance needed—just accurate times
  * **Unfamiliar trips (e.g., visiting family):** Wants **more guidance and reassurance** (wayfinding support, clarity)

* **Accessibility awareness:** Notes that her grandmother has very different needs (clear signage, physical accessibility, no app use), highlighting a **design challenge across user types**.

* **Key takeaway:** For Sofie, **reliability beats everything**. A simple system that delivers **honest arrival times and clear cancellation info** is far more valuable than advanced features.
