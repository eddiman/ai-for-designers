## 1. Trust in real-time information is the primary design problem

**Insight:** Users are not primarily struggling with bus delays—they are struggling with not being able to trust the information they receive.

### Evidence

**Observed**

* Interview participants repeatedly described incorrect countdowns, "ghost buses," and unreliable arrival predictions. *(Maja – interview-summaries.md; Geir – interview-summaries.md; Sofie – interview-summaries.md)*
* 55% of questionnaire respondents said they do not trust real-time information. *(questionnaire-summary.md)*
* Frustration categories were dominated by timing-related issues:
  * 31% "don't know if the bus is coming"
  * 26% schedule mismatch
  * 17% incorrect real-time information
    *(questionnaire-summary.md)*

### Inferred

* The biggest opportunity is not providing more information, but providing information users believe is accurate enough to act on.

***

## 2. Uncertainty creates more pain than delay

**Insight:** Users can tolerate delays when they understand what is happening, but uncertainty prevents confident decision-making.

### Evidence

**Observed**

* Interviewees explicitly stated that "not knowing" was worse than waiting. *(Maja, Geir, Sofie – interview-summaries.md)*
* Participants described anxiety and poor decisions caused by uncertainty. *(interview-summaries.md)*
* 79% of questionnaire respondents said they primarily want to reliably know when the bus will arrive. *(questionnaire-summary.md)*
* Open-text questionnaire responses highlighted waiting without information as particularly frustrating. *(questionnaire-summary.md)*

### Inferred

* Design should focus on reducing uncertainty and helping users make confident decisions rather than only optimizing for speed or punctuality.

***

## 3. Users are already compensating for system failures

**Insight:** People have developed workarounds because they do not trust the transport system's information.

### Evidence

**Observed**

* Maja checks multiple information sources. *(interview-summaries.md)*
* Sofie adds buffer time to avoid being late. *(interview-summaries.md)*
* Geir distrusts displays after previous bad experiences. *(interview-summaries.md)*
* Interviews revealed behaviors such as leaving early, taking earlier buses, and double-checking information. *(interview-summaries.md)*

### Inferred

* These behaviors represent hidden costs imposed on users (extra time, effort, and stress).
* Success should be measured partly by reducing the need for these compensating behaviors.

***

## 4. Different users need different levels of assistance

**Insight:** There is no single ideal interface; users range from simplicity-first to highly proactive information seekers.

### Evidence

**Observed**

* Maja expressed interest in a personalized, proactive assistant that provides notifications and guidance. *(interview-summaries.md)*
* Geir preferred simple, minimal solutions and explicitly rejected additional app complexity. *(interview-summaries.md)*
* Sofie also favored simplicity but wanted support when trips become unfamiliar. *(interview-summaries.md)*
* Questionnaire respondents showed mixed attitudes toward apps:
  * 34% positive
  * 38% negative
    *(questionnaire-summary.md)*

### Inferred

* The design should not assume all users want an advanced app experience.
* A layered approach may be needed: simple trusted information for everyone, with optional proactive assistance for users who want it.

***

## 5. The real problem includes cognitive and emotional burden, not just information access

**Insight:** Users expend mental effort trying to predict, verify, and monitor their journeys because they cannot rely on the system.

### Evidence

**Observed**

* Maja described feeling like she had to "mentally run the system" and reconstruct journeys herself. *(interview-summaries.md)*
* Interviews surfaced stress, anxiety, and frustration associated with travel uncertainty. *(interview-summaries.md)*
* Questionnaire results confirm widespread frustration, but interviews reveal the emotional depth behind it. *(questionnaire-summary.md; interview-summaries.md)*

### Inferred

* The design opportunity is not merely providing arrival data; it is reducing cognitive load and emotional stress.
* A successful solution should make users feel reassured and confident, not just informed.

***

## Ranked by strength of supporting evidence

1. **Trust in real-time information is the primary design problem** ✅ Strongest support (all interviews + questionnaire)
2. **Uncertainty creates more pain than delay** ✅ Strong support (all interviews + questionnaire)
3. **Users are already compensating for system failures** ✅ Strong interview support, reinforced by trust findings
4. **Different users need different levels of assistance** ✅ Supported by interview segmentation plus questionnaire attitudes
5. **The real problem includes cognitive and emotional burden** ✅ Strong qualitative support, weaker quantitative confirmation

Taken together, these point toward a design direction of **"help people make confident travel decisions through trustworthy information, while minimizing effort and supporting different levels of involvement."**
