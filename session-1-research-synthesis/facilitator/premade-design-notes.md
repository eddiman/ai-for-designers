# Session 1 research pack — design notes (do not share with participants)

This explains how the research pack in `premade/` was built and where the tension lives, so the facilitator can run the opening framing and the "a tension has been planted, but I won't say where" prompt. Sharing it with the room would defeat the cross-reference exercise.

## How the data was generated

All of it is synthetic, written for this course. The research question used is the broad framing from the plan: **"How do people plan and catch buses today, and where does it go wrong?"** — deliberately open, so the solution direction surfaces as a finding rather than being baked into the question. The questionnaire numbers and the interview quotes were constructed together to be internally consistent and to support the tension below.

## Where the tension is planted

**Questionnaire (the broad, shallow signal) → reliability and trust.**
- Q3 ranks "accurate, real-time arrival information" first at 47%; with "more frequent / reliable service" (21%) the reliability cluster is about two-thirds of respondents.
- Q4 "I just want to reliably know when my bus will arrive, without extra features" — 79% agree or strongly agree.
- Q6 "I trust the real-time information I currently get" — only 23% positive, 55% negative. Low trust is the headline opportunity, and it is about accuracy, not features.
- Q5 appetite for a feature-rich personal app — only 34% positive, 38% negative, 28% neutral. Real but a minority.

**Interviews (the narrow, deep signal) → one vocal app advocate among three.**
- **Maja** wants a full personal app: saved routes, favourites, proactive notifications, calendar awareness, "an app that knows me." She is the most articulate and the most quotable.
- **Geir** wants the basic information to be correct and actively rejects accounts, saved routes, and notifications as overkill.
- **Sofie** wants accurate, trustworthy times above all, is lukewarm on a feature-heavy app, and would skip the setup. Her one app-flavoured wish is a cancellation alert.

So the app advocate is one of three — a vocal minority — while two of three echo the questionnaire's reliability signal.

## The trap, and how it ties to the prompt-framing lesson

Maja's transcript is vivid and full of features, so a careless synthesis over-weights it and concludes "build the full personal app." A **directed** prompt ("…focusing on what they wish existed") amplifies this: it pulls Maja's wishlist to the front, and can file Sofie's single reliability-flavoured wish (cancellation alerts) under "app / notifications," inflating the app signal further. A **generic** prompt tends to come back blander but more balanced. The questionnaire is the corrective — the broad signal says most people want the basics done right and to be able to trust the information.

The cross-reference step (interview summaries vs. questionnaire summary) is where this should surface. Expected shape:
- **Agree:** real-time accuracy and trust matter to almost everyone (Geir, Sofie, and the questionnaire majority).
- **Conflict:** the interviews can read as "people want a rich personal app" if Maja dominates, while the questionnaire says appetite for that is a minority taste.
- **Gaps:** unfamiliar-city journeys (Sofie), accessibility for less digital riders (Sofie's grandmother), crowding at peak (Maja), and service disruptions/diversions (Geir) — each touched once and not measured in the questionnaire.

## The defensible tension to carry forward

Does the value sit in **reliability** (do the basics well for everyone) or in **personalisation** (build a rich app for engaged users like Maja)? That is the thread that runs through the course and becomes the Session 4 MVP question: what to build first.
