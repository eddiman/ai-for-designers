# Interview 2 — Geir

**Participant:** Geir, 54
**Occupation:** Warehouse supervisor
**Bus usage:** About three times a week into the city centre; drives otherwise
**Recorded:** 2026-05-21, ~30 min, semi-structured (excerpt)
**Interviewer:** facilitator (shown as **I**)

---

**I:** Thanks for sitting down with me. Can you tell me how you usually use the bus?

**Geir:** It's pretty simple for me. I drive most places, but when I go into the centre — appointments, the market on Saturdays, that sort of thing — I take the bus, because parking in town is a nightmare and it's expensive. So it's the same one route really. The 9 from the bottom of my road, straight in.

**I:** Always the same route?

**Geir:** Same route, more or less the same times. I'm a creature of habit. I'm not planning complicated journeys. I go in, I come back. That's the extent of it.

**I:** How do you check when it's coming?

**Geir:** There's a paper timetable in the shelter, and there's one of those electronic signs at the bigger stop. I look at the sign. My wife keeps telling me to put an app on, but I tried one and it was more hassle than it was worth. Honestly I don't want another app on my phone telling me things. I just want to know: is it coming, and when.

**I:** Does the sign do that for you?

**Geir:** When it's right, yes. The problem is it's not always right. It'll say four minutes, and four minutes comes and goes, and now it says one minute, and you're standing there like, well, which is it. Or it shows a bus that just never turns up. That's the bit that wears you down — not the waiting, the not knowing whether to trust the thing in front of you.

**I:** So it's a trust issue with the information.

**Geir:** That's exactly it. I don't need it to be clever. I need it to be *right.* If the sign said "the bus is genuinely going to be here in seven minutes" and that was true every time, I'd have no complaints at all. I'd never think about it again. All this other stuff people want, I don't know. I just want the basic fact to be correct.

**I:** Your wife mentioned an app — what put you off it?

**Geir:** It wanted me to make an account, set up favourites, allow notifications, all of that. For one bus route I take three times a week? It's overkill. I'm not against technology, I use my phone fine. But I felt like it was designed for somebody whose whole life is buses. That's not me. I want to open something, see "9, six minutes," and put my phone back in my pocket. Done.

**I:** So saved routes, notifications — those don't appeal?

**Geir:** Not really. I know my route. I don't need it saved, I'm not going to forget it. And notifications — no, please, the last thing I want is more pinging. My phone's busy enough. If anything I'd want *less.* The whole appeal of the bus for me is that it's simple. The moment the tool around it gets complicated, you've lost the point.

**I:** Tell me about a time the bus let you down.

**Geir:** There was a Saturday, I'm heading to the market, sign says the 9 is due, I wait, nothing. Waited twenty-odd minutes in the end. Found out later there'd been a diversion, roadworks, and the buses were all over the place. Nobody told us anything. There was no notice at the stop, the sign just kept showing times that weren't happening. If somebody had stuck a sign up, or the screen had just said "diversion today, expect delays," I'd have driven in instead and saved myself the morning. It's the being kept in the dark.

**I:** What would have made that better?

**Geir:** Just honesty from the system. Tell me what's actually going on. "It's running late." "It's cancelled." "There's a diversion." I'm a grown man, I can handle bad news, I can make my own plan. What I can't do anything with is a screen confidently telling me a bus is coming when it isn't.

**I:** If you could change one thing about catching the bus, what would it be?

**Geir:** Make the information you already give people actually true. That's it. Forget the bells and whistles. The sign, the timetable, whatever it is — if it just told the truth, that would fix ninety percent of it for me. Everything else is people overcomplicating a simple thing.

**I:** Anything else on your mind about it?

**Geir:** No, I think that's me. I'm probably a boring interview for you — I don't want much. But I suspect most people are like me, really. We just want the bus to turn up when it says, and to be told when it won't. That's not a lot to ask.
